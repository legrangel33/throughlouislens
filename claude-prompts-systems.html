Templates, Prompts & Systems: The Creator's Toolkit

22 min read · Updated May 2026 · throughlouislens

There is a version of the creator life where every week starts from zero. You sit down to make something and the first twenty minutes disappear deciding how to frame it, where to put it, what to write in the caption. That is not a creative problem. That is a systems problem. And it is completely fixable.

This guide is about building the toolkit that removes that friction. Templates for the structures you repeat. Prompt templates for the writing you delegate to AI. Systems that connect everything so content moves from idea to published post without you reinventing the wheel each time.

I use Notion, ClickUp, and Claude AI every day as a filmmaker, app builder, and content creator. What follows is what actually works.

Section 1: Why Templates and Systems Matter for Creators

The Real Cost of Starting From Scratch

Most creators underestimate how much time they lose to setup. Not to creation. To setup.

Every time you film a video without a shot list template, you miss angles you only remember in the edit. Every time you write a caption from scratch, you spend fifteen minutes finding a hook that a good prompt would have given you in ten seconds. Every time you post without a workflow, you publish the same platform five different ways depending on how you feel that day.

The cost is not just time. It is cognitive load. Decision fatigue is real, and it hits hardest before you start, which is exactly when you need the most creative energy.

A creator posting three times a week across two platforms is making around 300 content decisions per year. Templates and systems are how you take the repeatable 80% of those decisions off the table so you can focus on the 20% that actually requires original thinking.

The Difference Between a System and a Template

These two words get used interchangeably and they should not be.

A template is a reusable structure. It is a Notion database with the right properties already set up. It is a prompt you paste in and edit. It is a ClickUp task with pre-filled subtasks. A template is static until you use it.

A system is a repeatable process. It is the sequence of steps that turns an idea into a published post, every time, in the right order. A system tells you what happens next. It uses templates as its building blocks, but a system is the connective tissue between them.

You can have templates without a system (lots of good structures that sit unused). You can have a system without templates (a clear process that still requires you to rebuild pieces from scratch). What you want is both.

Set It Up Once, Run It on Autopilot

The goal is not to spend your life building systems. The goal is to spend a few hours building them well so you stop paying the setup tax every week.

This guide will take you through each layer: Notion for planning and knowledge, ClickUp for task execution, AI prompts for writing, and a full pipeline that connects all three. Build it once. Tweak it as you go. After that, it runs.

Section 2: Notion Templates

What Notion Is Good For

Notion is a workspace that sits somewhere between a document, a database, and a wiki. Its real strength is relational data: you can build databases that talk to each other, create filtered views of the same data, and link a content idea directly to the task tracking it and the analytics record measuring it.

For creators, Notion is the right home for:

  • Content planning. The ideas, the calendar, the pipeline
  • Knowledge management. Research, references, scripts, briefs
  • Publishing records. What went live, where, and when
  • Performance tracking. A running log of what is working

Notion is not great for task execution. It lacks the deadline urgency, automations, and notification system that ClickUp has. The division of labour that works: Notion holds the knowledge and the plan, ClickUp runs the execution.

The Content Creator OS: What It Should Contain

A Content Creator OS is a single Notion workspace that covers your entire content operation. Here is what it needs:

1. Ideas Database

Every idea you ever have goes here. Properties: idea title, content type (short-form video, long-form, carousel, etc.), platform, idea status (raw / developing / ready), and a notes field for context. The key is friction removal: it should take ten seconds to log an idea. Use a Notion quick-add button pinned to your sidebar or a simple mobile shortcut.

2. Content Calendar

A database view filtered to show upcoming content by publish date. This is the same underlying database as your publishing tracker. Just a different view. Properties: title, platform, publish date, format, status, linked idea, and a link to the draft or script.

3. Video Production Pipeline

A board or gallery view showing each piece of content moving through production stages. More on this below.

4. Publishing Tracker

A table or list view filtered to show everything that has gone live. Acts as your content archive. Cross-reference with an analytics log to track what is performing.

5. Analytics Log

A simple database with a record per post: title, platform, publish date, views, engagement, and a notes field for what you think drove performance. You do not need to obsess over numbers, but a monthly review of this log will tell you what to make more of.

How to Build or Use a Notion Template

Using a community template:

  1. Find a template on Notion's template gallery (notion.com/templates) or sites like notioneverything.com or productivetemply.com
  2. Click "Duplicate" on the template page. This copies it into your own workspace
  3. Rename it, adjust the properties to match your workflow, and delete any example data
  4. The database structure stays intact; you are just customising it

Customising properties:

Every Notion database has properties (columns). To edit them: open any item in the database, hover over a property name, and click the six-dot icon to edit, rename, or change the type. Common property types for creators: Select (for status or platform), Date (for publish date), Relation (to link to another database), URL (for links to drafts or live posts).

Creating filtered views:

One database, many views. In any Notion database, click "Add a view" and choose the layout (board, calendar, gallery, list, table). Then add filters: for example, a calendar view filtered to "Status = Published" shows only your live content mapped to when it went out. A gallery view filtered to "Status = Ready to Shoot" shows your immediate shoot queue.

This is the real power of Notion: you are not duplicating data, you are looking at the same data through different lenses.

Recommended Free Notion Templates for Creators

These are worth duplicating as a starting point and customising from there:

  • Notion's own Content Calendar template. Clean, minimal, good base. Find it at notion.com/templates/content-calendar
  • Content Planner (Notion Marketplace). Includes a content pipeline, idea bank, and multi-platform calendar. Find it at notion.com/templates/content-planner
  • Productive Temply Creator Templates. A curated list of templates specifically for content creators at productivetemply.com/blog/best-notion-templates-for-content-creators
  • Notion Everything. A broad repository of free Notion templates including creator OS builds at notioneverything.com/blog/free-notion-templates

Start with one. Do not try to build everything at once. A simple ideas database and a content calendar with five properties will get you further than an elaborate OS you spend a week building but never actually use.

Section 3: ClickUp Templates

What ClickUp Is Good For vs Notion

The clearest way to understand the difference: Notion stores what you know and plan, ClickUp tracks what you need to do and when.

ClickUp is built for task execution. It has real notifications, deadline tracking, time estimates, assignees, recurring tasks, and a robust automation engine. It is where work happens day to day.

For solo creators, ClickUp is the right home for:

  • Recurring content production tasks
  • Deadlines with actual reminders
  • Automation between stages
  • Time tracking per project (useful if you take on client work)
  • A content batch day checklist that resets itself weekly

If you also collaborate with an editor, a VA, or a brand partner, ClickUp's collaboration features make handoffs clear and trackable in a way Notion never quite manages.

Setting Up a Recurring Content Workflow

For solo creators who post on a weekly cadence, recurring tasks are one of the most underused ClickUp features.

Go to any task, click the recurrence option, and set it to weekly. When the task completes, ClickUp creates the next occurrence automatically. Use this for:

  • Weekly content planning session. Recurring every Monday
  • Batch filming day prep. Recurring before your scheduled shoot day
  • Analytics review. Recurring every Sunday evening
  • Caption and scheduling block. Recurring day before publish

The result: your recurring infrastructure is always waiting for you, not something you have to recreate.

Useful ClickUp Views for Creators

Board View (Kanban)

This is your daily driver. Each column is a status. Each card is a video or piece of content. Drag cards between columns as work progresses. At a glance you can see everything in editing, everything waiting to be filmed, and what is nearly ready.

Calendar View

Switch to this on Monday to see what is due this week. You can schedule tasks by due date, see publish dates clustered together, and quickly spot if you have a gap in output. Colour-code tasks by platform to see your cross-platform mix visually.

List View

Best for bulk editing. Filter by status, sort by due date, update multiple tasks at once. If you need to push back a batch of publish dates, this is faster than the board.

Table View

Useful for quarterly reviews. See all tasks in a spreadsheet-style layout, add custom fields as columns, and export data if you want to analyse it externally.

How Automations Save Hours Per Week

ClickUp Automations work on a trigger-action logic: when X happens, do Y. For creators, the most useful automations are:

Auto-move on status change

Trigger: Status changes to "Filming done" Action: Change status to "Editing" and notify editor (or yourself)

This means you never forget to update a task status after filming. You mark it done in the field, the pipeline moves itself.

Auto-assign when status changes

If you have a collaborator: Trigger: Status changes to "Review" Action: Assign task to reviewer

Due date nudge

Trigger: Due date is 48 hours away and status is not "Scheduled" Action: Send reminder notification

This catches anything that is at risk of missing a publish date.

Auto-create post-publish task

Trigger: Status changes to "Published" Action: Create a new linked task called "Log analytics, [video title]" due in 48 hours

This builds your analytics habit into the workflow rather than relying on you to remember.

You do not need all of these on day one. Pick the one automation that addresses your most consistent failure point and build from there.

Section 4: AI Prompt Templates

What a Prompt Template Is

A prompt template is a pre-written instruction you give to an AI that is structured so you only need to fill in one or two variables to get a useful output. Instead of writing a new prompt from scratch each time you need a script, you keep a master prompt that says "write a 60-second script for [TOPIC] using [HOOK TYPE]" and you just swap the brackets.

The difference in output quality between a rough prompt ("write me a video script about this") and a structured prompt is significant. Structured prompts consistently produce work that is closer to usable on the first pass, which means less editing and less frustration.

The Anatomy of a Good Claude Prompt

A well-built prompt has five components. You do not always need all five, but knowing them helps you diagnose why a prompt is not performing.

1. Role (optional but useful for specialist tasks) Tell Claude what perspective to take. "You are a short-form video scriptwriter who writes for TikTok and Instagram Reels." This sets the register and approach before the task begins. Skip it for simple tasks. It adds nothing when you just need a list.

2. Context What Claude needs to know before starting. Who you are, what the content is for, what platform it will live on, what your audience looks like, what you have already decided. Context prevents Claude from making assumptions that waste your time.

3. Task The actual instruction. State it clearly in the first sentence of the task block. "Write a 60-second script" is clearer than "I was thinking maybe you could write something like a script." One task per prompt. If you need a script and a caption, write two prompts or ask for them separately within the same conversation.

4. Format How you want the output structured. "Return the script with the hook on its own line, followed by body, followed by CTA. No introduction, just the script." Claude will default to whatever format seems right if you do not specify. Which is often not what you want.

5. Constraints What to avoid or keep within. "Keep it under 150 words. No hashtags. No rhetorical questions. Write in first person." Constraints are how you inject your voice and boundaries into the output.

The Claude documentation recommends using XML tags for longer, more complex prompts. Wrapping context in <context> tags and the task in <task> tags helps the model parse what is instruction and what is background. For simple prompts, plain language works fine.

5 Ready-to-Use Prompt Templates

These are prompts I actually use. Copy them into a document or your prompt library, fill in the variables, and adjust the constraints to your voice.

Prompt 1: Short-Form Video Script from a Topic and Hook

``` You are a short-form video scriptwriter. You write for TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts.

Context: I am a filmmaker and app builder who makes content about creativity, tools, and building things. My audience is creators and makers aged 20-35. My tone is direct and practical. No hype, no filler.

Task: Write a 60-second video script on the topic: [TOPIC]. Use this hook style: [HOOK TYPE. E.g. "provocative statement" / "counterintuitive fact" / "problem-first"]. The script should have a clear hook (first 3 seconds), a main point with one specific example, and a CTA that is a natural next step rather than a hard sell.

Format: Return the hook on its own line, labelled HOOK. Then the body text. Then the CTA on its own line, labelled CTA. No introduction. Just the script.

Constraints: Maximum 150 words. First person. No rhetorical questions. No phrases like "in today's world" or "game-changer." Write as if I am speaking directly to camera. ```

Prompt 2: Repurpose a Long Video into 3 Short-Form Hooks

``` Context: I have a [LENGTH]-minute video about [TOPIC]. Here is the rough transcript or summary: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT OR SUMMARY].

Task: Identify the three strongest standalone moments or insights from this video that would work as short-form hooks for TikTok or Reels. For each one, write: the hook line (first 3 seconds of the short), a one-sentence summary of what the clip covers, and a suggested on-screen caption (under 10 words).

Format: Return three numbered sections, each with HOOK, SUMMARY, and CAPTION clearly labelled.

Constraints: Hooks should be under 12 words. Write in first person. No questions as hooks. Each hook should be able to stand alone. The viewer should not need to have seen the original video to understand the short. ```

Prompt 3: Free Guide Outline from a Topic

``` Context: I create free guides for my audience of creators and makers. The guides are practical, beginner-accessible but not condescending, and written in a direct first-person voice. No buzzwords, no filler.

Task: Write a detailed outline for a free guide titled: [GUIDE TITLE]. The outline should cover [NUMBER] sections. Each section should include: a section heading, 4-6 bullet points listing the specific sub-topics or practical points to cover, and one sentence explaining the purpose of that section within the overall guide.

Format: Return each section as a numbered heading with bulleted sub-points underneath. Include a one-sentence section purpose note in italics under each heading.

Constraints: This is a written guide, not a video script. Aim for comprehensive coverage, not brevity. The outline should give a writer enough structure to write 2,000-4,000 words from it without having to make structural decisions. ```

Prompt 4: Instagram Caption from a Video Description

``` Context: I am posting a short-form video to Instagram. My account is @throughlouislens. A creator brand about filmmaking, building apps with AI, and systems thinking. My captions are direct, specific, and personal. I do not write fluffy captions.

Task: Write an Instagram caption for this video: [PASTE VIDEO DESCRIPTION OR 2-3 SENTENCE SUMMARY OF WHAT THE VIDEO COVERS].

Format: The caption should open with a one or two sentence hook that does not start with "I" and does not start with a question. Follow with 3-5 sentences of substance. Close with a single call-to-action line. Add 5 relevant hashtags on a new line at the end.

Constraints: Maximum 220 words. No emojis in the caption body (emojis only in the CTA line if appropriate). No phrases like "hit the link in bio". Be more specific. Write as if this is a real person speaking, not a brand account. ```

Prompt 5: Build a Notion Database Structure from a Workflow Description

``` Context: I use Notion to manage my content and projects. I want to set up a new database to support the following workflow: [DESCRIBE YOUR WORKFLOW IN 3-5 SENTENCES. What you are tracking, who is involved, what the end result is].

Task: Design a Notion database structure for this workflow. For each database you recommend, list: the database name, the purpose, each property (name, type, and what it tracks), the recommended views (calendar, board, table, etc.) and what filters to apply to each, and any relationships to other databases.

Format: Return each database as a numbered section. Use a table format for the properties list: Property Name | Property Type | What It Tracks.

Constraints: Keep the structure lean. Only include properties I will actually fill in. If something could be tracked in a note field rather than a separate property, say so. Do not recommend paid Notion features. Assume I am on the free plan. ```

How to Save Prompts as Reusable Skills in Claude Cowork

If you use Claude's Cowork environment, you can save any prompt as a skill. A reusable, callable instruction set that lives in your workspace. The practical benefit: instead of pasting a long prompt every time, you type a skill command and the prompt fires immediately.

To build a skill in Claude Cowork:

  1. Write and test your prompt until it reliably produces good output
  2. In Cowork, open the Skills panel and create a new skill
  3. Paste your prompt as the skill's instruction, leaving the variables as clearly labelled placeholders
  4. Name the skill with a short command you will actually remember
  5. Add a one-line description so you remember what it does

The key discipline: treat your skills like code. When a prompt stops performing or you find a better version, update the skill rather than creating a new one. Keep the library small and current rather than accumulating dozens of outdated prompts.

Building a Personal Prompt Library

Whether you use Cowork skills or not, keep a prompt library somewhere. A Notion database works well: one row per prompt, with properties for name, category (scripts, captions, outlines, databases, etc.), last tested date, and a rating field so you can see which ones you actually trust.

A few principles for maintaining it:

  • Date every prompt. AI models update. A prompt that worked brilliantly six months ago might need adjustment for the current model.
  • Log what does not work. Keep a "retired prompts" section with notes on why something stopped performing. This prevents you from re-trying the same broken approach.
  • Iterate by variable. When a prompt is nearly right but not quite, change one element at a time. The role, the constraint, the format. Rather than rewriting the whole thing. This way you know what actually changed the output.
  • Share the ones that work. If a prompt reliably produces strong output, it is worth documenting and sharing. Your prompt library is a competitive asset.

Section 5: The "Shoot Once, Publish Many" System

The Core Idea

The biggest time waste in content creation is not filming. It is everything around filming: planning it, scripting it, captioning it, reformatting it for each platform, and tracking whether it went out. A well-designed system collapses that overhead dramatically.

The "shoot once, publish many" approach starts from a simple premise: one filming session should produce multiple pieces of content, and one piece of content should appear on multiple platforms with minimal extra work. This is not about recycling mediocre content. It is about being intentional with what you capture so it serves more than one purpose.

Research consistently shows that creators with active repurposing workflows save 60-80% of creation time compared to starting from scratch for each platform. At three posts per week across two platforms, that is a meaningful return.

The Full Workflow: From Idea to Published Post

Here is how Notion, ClickUp, and your AI prompts connect into a single pipeline.

Phase 1: Capture (Notion)

An idea lands. You open Notion on your phone and add a row to your Ideas database in under thirty seconds. Title, rough concept, platform. Nothing more. The idea is captured; it is not lost to the void.

Phase 2: Development (Notion + AI)

When an idea is ready to develop, open it in Notion and start a linked Notion Doc for the script and brief. Use your short-form script prompt template to get a first draft from Claude. Paste the output into the Notion Doc, edit it to your voice, and mark the idea status as "Ready to Shoot."

Phase 3: Production Kickoff (ClickUp)

When a piece of content moves to "Ready to Shoot" in Notion, create a corresponding task in ClickUp (or use a linked automation if you have set one up). The ClickUp task loads your pre-built subtask checklist. Set the due date. The task enters your production pipeline at the "Ready to Film" status.

Phase 4: Filming (ClickUp)

On your batch day (see below), work through all tasks in "Ready to Film" status. As you finish each one, update the ClickUp status to "Filming Done." If you have set up the auto-status automation, the task moves to "Editing" automatically and you get a notification.

Phase 5: Post-Production (ClickUp + AI)

Edit in your tool of choice. When the edit is done, use your Instagram caption prompt or TikTok hook prompt to generate caption drafts. Paste the final captions into the ClickUp task description or attach them as a note. Move status to "Ready to Post."

Phase 6: Publishing (ClickUp + Notion)

Schedule the post on your platform or publish directly. Update the ClickUp task status to "Published." In Notion, mark the content calendar entry as published and log the publish date and URL.

Phase 7: Analytics (ClickUp)

If you set up the auto-create analytics task automation, a task already exists to log performance at 48 hours. Open it, pull the numbers from the platform, and paste them into your Notion Analytics Log. Close the task.

That is the full loop. Every piece of content follows the same path. Nothing gets lost, nothing gets skipped, and you never have to remember where you are in the process.

What a Content Batch Day Looks Like

Batching is the production philosophy that makes this system viable for a solo creator. Rather than filming one video, editing it, captioning it, and posting it before moving to the next. You do all of the planning for the week in one block, all of the filming in one block, and all of the editing and scheduling in one block.

This works because each type of work has a different mental mode. Scripting is writing brain. Filming is performance brain. Editing is technical and aesthetic brain. Switching between them constantly is expensive. Batching lets you go deep into each mode once.

A typical batch week might look like this:

Monday (30-45 minutes): Planning block

Open ClickUp. Check the pipeline. Review what is in "Ready to Shoot." Open Notion and review the Ideas database. Decide what the week's content will be. Use AI prompts to develop any scripts that need drafting. By end of this session: two or three pieces of content have scripts, shot lists, and ClickUp tasks with due dates set.

Wednesday (2-3 hours): Filming block

Shoot everything that is in "Ready to Film." If you are doing three short-form videos, this is 2-3 hours including setup and teardown. One set of kit, one lighting setup, batch the talking head content, then batch the B-roll. Think in advance about outfit changes or location variations so the batch does not look like one session. Update ClickUp statuses as you go.

Thursday/Friday (2-4 hours): Edit and schedule block

Edit all three pieces. Use AI caption prompts to draft captions while the exports run. Schedule or upload to each platform. Update Notion with publish dates and URLs. Set the 48-hour analytics reminders.

Sunday evening (15 minutes): Analytics review

Log the numbers. Note one thing that worked and one thing to test next week.

Total active time: roughly six to eight hours per week for three pieces of content across two platforms. Without a system, the same output routinely takes twelve to fifteen hours because of context switching, forgetting where things are, and recreating work you have done before.

A Note on Starting

If you are reading this and you do not currently have any of this set up, do not try to build it all at once.

Pick one section that addresses your most pressing pain point and build that first. If you are losing ideas before they become content, start with the Notion Ideas database. If your videos are getting stuck in editing because there is no clear "next step," start with the ClickUp pipeline. If you are spending too long writing captions, start with two of the AI prompt templates.

The system compounds over time. The more consistently you use it, the faster it gets and the less you have to think about the infrastructure. The goal is to get to a place where the system handles the mechanics of content production and you spend your energy on the only part that cannot be templated: making things worth watching.
