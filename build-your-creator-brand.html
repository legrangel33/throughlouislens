Build Your Creator Brand in a Weekend

19 min read · Updated May 2026 · throughlouislens

Most creator branding advice is theoretical. It tells you what to think about without telling you what to actually do. This guide is a 2-day action plan. By the end of Sunday, you will have a name, a visual identity, a Notion workspace, your core templates, and your first five pieces of content planned. Not perfect. Done. Done beats perfect every time when you're starting out.

1. Before You Start

Clear your schedule for two days. Not "I'll do it when I have a moment". Two actual days where this is the thing you're working on. You will not need expensive tools. You will need Canva (free), Notion (free), a camera or decent phone, and access to your existing content or ideas.

The goal of this weekend is not to build a finished, polished brand. The goal is to build enough of a brand that you can start publishing without being held back by decisions that haven't been made yet. Speed of shipping is more valuable than perfection of design at this stage.

One mindset point: don't try to build the brand you aspire to. Build the brand that reflects what you actually make right now. You can evolve it. You cannot fake it.

2. Day 1 Morning: Define What You Actually Do

This is the most important session of the weekend and most people rush it. Don't.

The One-Sentence Framework

Your brand needs a single sentence that explains what you make and who it's for. Not a mission statement. Not a vision. One clear sentence that tells a stranger exactly what they'll find if they follow you.

The format I use:

"I make [format] about [topic] for [audience]."

Examples of this done well:

  • "I make cinematic travel videos about sailing for people who want to live differently."
  • "I make honest gear reviews for photographers who shoot on a budget."
  • "I make short tutorials about iPhone filmmaking for creators who don't want to carry a camera."

Examples of this done wrong:

  • "I create content about life, travel, and creativity." (too vague. Who is this for?)
  • "I'm a storyteller and visual artist." (says nothing about what you actually make)
  • "I document my journey as an entrepreneur and creator." (about you, not for your audience)

Spend 30 minutes on this. Write 10 versions. Pick the one that is most specific and most honest. If you genuinely cannot write this sentence, that is important information. It means your niche is not defined yet and you need to work on that before anything else. Do not design a logo before you know what you're making.

Niche Clarity Test

Ask yourself three questions:

  1. What is the specific topic I can talk about for years without running out of things to say?
  2. Who is the specific person I'm making this for? (Age, situation, what they want, what frustrates them)
  3. What do I have that they can't easily get elsewhere? (Experience, access, perspective, style)

Your answers to these three questions are the brief for your entire brand. Write them down. They inform every decision from here.

3. Day 1 Midday: Choose Your Name and Handle

Your name and handle are not just identity. They are infrastructure. A handle change later is painful. Platform URLs change, links break, audience recognition resets. Get this right now.

Criteria for a Good Creator Handle

Available across all major platforms. Check Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, Twitter/X, and Pinterest simultaneously. Use Namecheckr or similar tools to check multiple platforms at once. If the handle is taken on two or more platforms, move on.

Pronounceable and spellable. If someone hears your handle in a podcast recommendation, can they type it correctly the first time? "throughlouislens" passes this test. It's a phrase, each word is common. "xllvzwcreates" fails. "l0uisvlogs" (with a zero) fails. Make it easy to find.

Not locked into a trend or moment. Avoid incorporating a current platform name, a trend year, or a buzzword that has a shelf life. "TikTokTravels2024" was wrong the moment it was created. "ViralVideosLouis" is embarrassing in 18 months.

Reflects you or your content, not just keywords. SEO on social media is less important than recognition. A name that people remember and recommend is worth more than a name that ranks for a keyword.

Short enough to tag. Under 20 characters where possible. Long handles get truncated in thumbnails, profile cards, and watermarks.

If You Can't Find Anything Available

Common workarounds: add "the" at the front (not ideal but workable), add your country code or city if it's relevant to your content, use your real name if it's available and you're comfortable building around it. Do not add random numbers unless the number means something (birth year is acceptable, "428" added because everything else was taken is not).

4. Day 1 Afternoon: Brand Colours

Two to three colours maximum. One primary. One secondary. One optional accent. More than this and your visual identity becomes inconsistent across different applications.

How to Pick Your Colours

Pull from your actual content, not a palette generator. Open your best 10-15 photos or video frames. The work you're most proud of and most representative of what you make. Look at the dominant colours. Look at what the light looks like, what the environments look like, what colour you appear to be drawn to. Your brand colour should not be a surprise to anyone who has watched your content.

For me, the visual tone of my work. The tones I gravitate toward in colour grading. Directly informed the palette I built around. That connection is what makes a brand feel coherent rather than designed.

Test your colours on real backgrounds. A colour that looks sharp in a palette generator looks different on a white Instagram grid, on a dark YouTube banner, and on a black thumbnail background. Before committing, test your primary colour against black, white, and mid-grey. It needs to read clearly on all three.

Accessibility matters. If your brand colour palette includes a light colour for text, make sure it has sufficient contrast against the backgrounds you'll use it on. White text on pale yellow is hard to read. This is especially relevant for thumbnail text.

Get the hex codes. Once you have your two or three colours decided, note the exact hex codes and store them in your Notion brand assets database. Every design tool you use. Canva, Final Cut title templates, any website builder. Accepts hex codes. Consistency depends on using the exact same values every time.

The Most Common Mistake

Picking colours because they look good together in the abstract rather than because they reflect your work. A muted terracotta and warm off-white might look beautiful in a brand identity presentation, but if your content is tech reviews shot on a white desk under harsh LED, there is a visual disconnect your audience will feel even if they can't articulate it.

5. Day 1 Late: Typography

Typography is simpler than most creators make it. You need two fonts: one for headings and display text, one for body copy. That's it.

Finding Free Fonts

Google Fonts. Free, high-quality, widely supported. Go to fonts.google.com and browse. Filter by category (Serif, Sans Serif, Display) and test your brand name in the preview field. You're looking for fonts that match the energy of your content. Minimal and modern, bold and editorial, warm and approachable, technical and precise. Pick what fits the tone, not what looks trendy.

Font pairing that works. A few reliable combinations:

  • Inter + Merriweather. Clean, readable, works for almost any content category. Inter for headings, Merriweather for body.
  • Playfair Display + Lato. More editorial and premium-feeling. Good for lifestyle, travel, fashion adjacent content.
  • Space Grotesk + Inter. Modern, tech-adjacent, clean. Works well for tech creators, app developers, software reviewers.
  • DM Sans + DM Serif Display. Versatile, contemporary. Good all-round choice if you want something that doesn't skew too hard in any direction.

The rule: one font with personality (your display/heading font), one font that is clean and readable (your body font). Don't pick two expressive fonts. They will fight each other.

Where you'll actually use these. Canva thumbnails, Instagram post text, YouTube end cards, any website or link-in-bio page, and any overlay text in your video edits. You don't need to implement this everywhere immediately. Just commit to the choices so they're consistent from the first piece of content you publish.

6. Day 1 Evening: Write Your Bio

Your bio is the hardest piece of writing you'll do this weekend. It needs to do four things in four sentences or fewer: tell people who you are, what you make, who it's for, and why you're different.

The Bio Formula

[Who you are] + [what you make] + [who it's for] + [one specific differentiator]

Working through each element:

Who you are. Your name and one factual anchor. Location, background, role, or relevant credential. Keep it brief. "Louis Le Grange. Filmmaker and creator based in France" is enough. You don't need to list every title you've ever held.

What you make. This is your one-sentence answer from the morning session. Drop it in.

Who it's for. Name them specifically. "For solo creators who want to shoot better video without spending more money." "For people planning a sailing trip with no experience." The more specific, the better. Specific audiences feel seen, vague audiences feel ignored.

One differentiator. What is one true thing about your content or perspective that is not generic? Not "I'm passionate about what I do". That is not a differentiator. "I've been shooting on iPhones exclusively for three years and I don't own a cinema camera" is a differentiator. "I test every piece of gear I review against my own money" is a differentiator. "I learned to sail at 34 with no background and I document what that actually looks like" is a differentiator.

Platform length adjustments. Instagram bio: 150 characters maximum, so compress hard. YouTube about: 1-2 paragraphs, you have room to expand. Twitter/X: 160 characters, keep it to the sharpest one or two elements. Notion or website: full version.

Write the full version first, then compress for each platform.

7. Day 1 End: Set Up Your Notion Workspace

Your Notion workspace is your operating system for the brand. Setting it up properly now saves significant time later and means you're never starting a content session by asking yourself where things are supposed to live.

The Four Databases You Actually Need

Content Calendar. Properties: Title, Status (Idea / In Progress / Filmed / Editing / Scheduled / Published), Platform, Format (Short / Long-form / Post / Story), Publish Date, Content Pillar. This is your production view. Everything you're making lives here from idea to publish.

Ideas Backlog. A simple database with Title, Source (observation, comment, trend, question), Date Added, Priority (High / Medium / Someday). The rule: every content idea goes here immediately when you have it, even if you'll never use it. The act of capturing stops you losing ideas and stops you second-guessing yourself mid-production.

Brand Assets. A reference page (not a database. Just a structured page) with your hex codes, font names and download links, logo file (once you have one), handle on each platform, and bio versions for each platform. This is the source of truth. When you need to set up a new platform or send a brand kit to someone, everything is here.

Gear List. Properties: Item Name, Category (Camera / Audio / Lighting / Storage / Accessories), Status (Own / Wishlist / Sold), Purchase Date, Purchase Price. This doubles as a reference when you're writing gear review content and as a practical record of what you actually own.

What to Skip at This Stage

Don't build an elaborate analytics tracking system. Don't create a publishing SOP document. Don't set up a client management database. You don't have enough content history or client volume to populate any of those things yet. Build them when you need them. Right now, the four databases above are everything you need to start working.

8. Day 2 Morning: Visual Templates in Canva

Day 2 starts with building the templates you'll use for every piece of content. The goal is to create these once, properly, and then never redesign them. Just swap in new images and text.

Thumbnail Template

For YouTube and Shorts, your thumbnail is your most important visual asset. Build one template and use it consistently.

What goes in the template: A background layer (either a strong single colour, a gradient, or a placeholder for a photo), a text layer for the title in your heading font, and optionally your name or logo in a corner. Keep it to three elements maximum.

Canva setup: Create a custom size 1280 x 720px for YouTube, or 1080 x 1920px for Shorts/Reels. Set your background using your primary brand colour. Add a text element in your heading font in your secondary or accent colour. Save this as a template in Canva. For each new video, duplicate the template, swap the background photo if you're using one, and update the text. Done in 5 minutes.

What makes a good thumbnail text: Clear benefit or curiosity gap stated in under 6 words. Large enough to read on a phone screen (minimum 80pt, test by zooming out on the Canva preview). High contrast between text and background.

Instagram Post Template

Build two versions: one for quote or text-based posts, one for photo posts with overlay text. Same principles as the thumbnail. Your fonts, your colours, consistent placement.

Reel Cover Template

Instagram Reels need a cover image. What viewers see before they play. This is often ignored and it shouldn't be. A clear, consistent Reel cover makes your profile grid look intentional. Create a 1080 x 1920px template with your branding applied.

9. Day 2 Midday: Profile Photos and Cover Images

Your profile photo is your most recurring visual across every platform. It appears in every comment you leave, every recommendation someone sees, every tag. It needs to be good.

Profile Photo Criteria

Consistent across all platforms. The exact same photo on YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, and anywhere else you're present. No exceptions. The recognition value of seeing the same face in the same framing reinforces your identity every time someone encounters you across different contexts.

Your face, front and centre. Logos as profile photos work for established brands. For a creator building from scratch, a clear photo of your face builds faster trust and recognition. You are the brand at this stage.

Good light, neutral background. You don't need a studio. Natural window light in a room with a relatively clean background is enough. The photo should be cropped to head and shoulders. Your face should take up at least half the frame.

Not a thumbnail, not a landscape. Profile photos are displayed in a circle and at very small sizes. A landscape photo with you as a small figure in the frame looks terrible at profile photo scale. Close crop, clear face.

Shoot three to five options. Don't commit to the first one you take. Try slightly different light, slightly different angles, slightly different expressions. Pick the one that looks most like you and most like the energy of your content.

Cover Images

YouTube banner: 2560 x 1440px. This appears differently on desktop, tablet, and mobile. Canva's YouTube banner template shows the safe zone. Keep all text and important visual elements within the central safe zone.

Twitter/X banner: 1500 x 500px.

The same visual language applies: your brand colours, your fonts if you include text, and imagery consistent with your content category.

10. Day 2 Afternoon: Content Pillars

Content pillars are the 3-5 topic areas you'll build your content around. They give you a framework for deciding what to make and they give your audience a reason to stay subscribed because they know what to expect.

How to Pick Yours

Your pillars should meet three criteria:

  1. You can talk about this topic indefinitely without running out of things to say
  2. It connects naturally to your defined niche
  3. Your target audience is actively interested in this topic

For a creator making content about iPhone filmmaking for solo creators, pillars might be: gear and kit, shooting technique, editing workflow, post-production tools, and building a content business. Every piece of content fits into one of those buckets.

Test Them in the First 30 Days

Pick your pillars now, but treat them as hypotheses. In the first 30 days, make content across all 5. Watch which pillars get the most engagement, saves, and shares. What people respond to is more reliable data than what you think they want. After 30 days, double down on the 2-3 that are working. The pillars that consistently underperform can be reduced or dropped.

11. Day 2 Late Afternoon: Your First 5 Content Pieces

The biggest paralysis for new creators is not knowing what to make first. Here is a framework that solves it.

What Not to Make First

Do not make a "Hi, I'm new here" introduction video. Nobody is looking for you yet. An introduction video has no searchable value, no shareability, and no benefit to a viewer who finds you via a recommendation. It only matters to people who already follow you. And you have no followers yet.

What to Make First

Your first 5 pieces should each demonstrate a specific, concrete piece of value in your niche. They should stand alone as useful content even if someone has never seen anything else you've made. They should be representative of the type of content you're committing to producing.

A framework for the first 5:

  1. The most common beginner mistake in your niche. And what to do instead. High search intent, high shareability, immediately positions you as someone who knows what they're talking about.
  2. Your honest take on a specific tool, piece of gear, or method in your niche. Not "Top 10 cameras." One specific thing. Your actual opinion.
  3. A practical tutorial or walkthrough. Step by step, showing someone how to do something specific. Tutorials have long-term search value.
  4. A personal piece. A short, specific story from your own experience in your niche. Not a life story. One specific moment, what happened, what you learned. This is how people connect to you rather than just the information.
  5. A response to a common question in your niche. Go to Reddit, YouTube comments, or forums in your space and find the question that gets asked over and over. Answer it thoroughly.

Plan all 5 in your Content Calendar in Notion before you start filming any of them. Having a plan prevents the "what do I make next?" paralysis from setting in after the first piece.

12. Day 2 Evening: Link-in-Bio and Email Hook

Link-in-Bio

You need one URL to share everywhere. Options in order of my preference:

Linktree (free). Quickest to set up. Functional. Looks slightly generic. Good for getting started. Custom domain is available on paid tier if you want it to look more like your own.

Notion public page. If you already know Notion, this is worth doing. Create a simple page with your name, one-line bio, and links to your platforms and any resources you're offering. Toggle on "Share to web." It looks cleaner than a default Linktree and it's a chance to extend your brand into another touchpoint.

Custom website. Worth building eventually, not this weekend. Keep it off the to-do list for now.

Whatever you choose: keep it simple. Name, brief description, 3-5 links max. Latest content, your most important platform, an email sign-up. Don't build a complex site when you have no content yet.

Email Sign-Up Hook

Set up an email list from day one. Not when you have 1,000 followers. Day one.

The platform does not matter much at this stage. ConvertKit (now Kit), Mailchimp, and Beehiiv are all workable. The free tiers on each are sufficient until you have a few hundred subscribers.

What matters is the hook. The specific reason someone should give you their email address. "Sign up for my newsletter" is not a hook. "Get my iPhone filmmaking settings as a PDF" is a hook. "Join 200 creators getting weekly gear breakdowns" is a hook (once you have 200, which you'll get to). For now: one specific, tangible thing someone gets when they subscribe. A resource, a checklist, a template, a guide. Create it this weekend while you're already in the brand-building mindset.

13. The Tools I Actually Use

Not the tools I use for affiliate income. The ones I actually use.

Notion. For all planning, content calendar, ideas, reference. Free tier is plenty for a solo creator. I use it daily.

Canva. For all graphics. Thumbnails, post templates, brand visuals, quick mockups. Free tier covers most needs. Canva Pro ($13/month) is worth it once you're producing regularly because the Brand Kit feature lets you lock in your fonts and colours so they're always accessible.

Claude. For ideation, script writing, bio drafts, research, idea expansion, and any writing task where I want a thinking partner or a faster first draft. I use this more than any other AI tool.

ElevenLabs. For voiceover on videos where I'm not recording my own audio, and for my cloned voice when I want to produce quickly without a recording session.

Final Cut Pro. For all video editing. One-time purchase, macOS only. If you're on Windows, DaVinci Resolve's free tier is the best alternative.

iPhone 17 Pro. Primary camera. ProRes + Apple Log. No dedicated cinema camera.

That is the complete stack for throughlouislens. Nothing else runs regularly.

14. Common Mistakes

Trying to look like someone else. The most common and most damaging mistake. You pick a creator you admire, you build a brand that looks like theirs, and you spend the next year trying to catch someone who has a multi-year head start by producing inferior versions of what they already make. Build something that looks like you. There is no competition for being yourself, but there are thousands of competitors for every established aesthetic you're trying to copy.

Picking a niche too broad. "Lifestyle" is not a niche. "Travel" is not a niche. "Technology" is not a niche. These are categories. "Budget travel in Southeast Asia with kids" is a niche. "AI tools for freelance writers" is a niche. "iPhone filmmaking for creators who don't want to carry a camera" is a niche. The broader your niche, the harder it is to build an audience because you're not speaking specifically to anyone.

Waiting until everything is perfect. There is no version of this where it's perfect before you start. Your first pieces of content will be imperfect. Your first templates will be wrong. Your first bio will change. Publish anyway. The feedback loop from real content performance is worth more than any amount of pre-launch planning. Waiting costs you months of data you could be collecting.

Using different handles on different platforms. This seems minor and it is not. When someone wants to tag you, they will try your Instagram handle first. If it doesn't match your YouTube handle, they won't bother finding the right one. Consistent handles across platforms are basic discoverability infrastructure. Do this at the start, not retroactively.

No email list from day one. Your social media followers are on rented land. The platform can change its algorithm, reduce your reach, ban your account, or simply stop growing. Your email list is the only audience you own. Even if you send one email a month, start collecting addresses from the first piece of content you publish. There is no such thing as "I'll start the email list later when I have more followers." Later is always too late.

15. What's Next After the Weekend

You've spent two days building the foundation. Now you execute.

The discipline that separates creators who build an audience from those who don't is consistency of publishing more than quality of production. Publish on a schedule you can sustain. One good video per week beats two great videos followed by three months of silence. Start with a frequency that is manageable and increase it when your workflow improves.

Review your content performance after the first 30 days. What performed? What didn't? Double down on what worked. Don't stick rigidly to pillars or formats that your audience isn't responding to just because you planned them. The plan was a starting point. Real data changes the plan.

Three months in, revisit your brand. Your colours might need a tweak. Your bio will almost certainly improve. Your Notion workspace will evolve. None of this is wasted. Every iteration is informed by real production experience rather than pre-launch theory.

The brand you build this weekend is not your final brand. It's your first real one.
